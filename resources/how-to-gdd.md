# How to write a GDD
---

A Game Design Document (GDD) is the foundational document that describes the complete vision of a video game. While there is no universal format, here is the most common structure used in the industry:

---

## GDD Structure

### 1. Overview / Vision Statement
A summary of the game in a few lines. It answers: *What is this game? Who is the audience? What is the core concept?*
It includes the title, genre, target platforms, tone (serious, humorous, dark…), and a 2-3 sentence pitch that captures the essence.

**USP (Unique Selling Point):** The answer to the question: *"Why play your game instead of another?"* This is perhaps the most strategic element of the document. It forces you to identify the real differentiator of your game right from the start.

### 2. Gameplay Core Loop
The heart of the document. It describes the fundamental cycle of actions the player repeats: what they do moment-to-moment, what their short- and long-term motivations are, and how actions chain together. Example: *explore → fight → gather resources → upgrade → explore harder areas.*
- **Core Mechanics** — movement, jumping, basic interaction.
- **Advanced Mechanics** — combos, skill trees, crafting, complex systems.

### 3. Game Mechanics
Every gameplay system explained in detail: controls and input, physics and movement, combat system (if applicable), character progression (levels, skill trees, stats), game economy (currencies, resources, crafting), and any other relevant mechanics. Each mechanic should explain **how it works**, **why it exists**, and **how it interacts with others**.

### 4. Content Structure
Maps out the game's content: levels, worlds, or zones; missions, quests, or stages; difficulty curve; and estimated duration of each section. Often includes a flowchart showing player progression.
- **Level Objectives:** Explicitly define what the player must do to complete each area (find an item, defeat a boss, reach a point). It seems obvious, but putting it down in black and white avoids massive ambiguity during development.
- **Structure Type:** Specifying whether levels are linear, open-world, or procedural is fundamental architectural information.

### 5. Characters
For every main character (protagonist, antagonist, important NPCs): name, narrative role, abilities or stats, character arc, and — if relevant — sketches or visual references.

### 6. Story and Narrative
The narrative context of the game: premise and world-building, main plot (without necessarily revealing everything), dialogue system, planned cutscenes, and how the narrative integrates with the gameplay (or if it remains separate).

### 7. Art Direction
The visual direction: reference graphic style (realistic, stylized, pixel art…), color palette, references to other games or works, interface and HUD, and visual effects. Includes mood boards and concept art if available.

### 8. Audio Design
Musical style and references, types of music for each context (exploration, combat, menus), sound design for key actions, and voice acting (yes/no, in which languages).

### 9. UI/UX
How the player interacts with the menus: navigation flow, main screens (main menu, pause, inventory…), in-game HUD, and accessibility features.

### 10. Target Audience & Monetization
Who the ideal player is (age, gaming experience, interests), business model (premium, free-to-play, subscription), and if applicable, the system for microtransactions or DLC.

### 11. Scope & Roadmap
What is **in** the game for version 1.0 (scope) and what is postponed, development timeline, main milestones, and the team involved.

---

## Practical Tips

**On Format:** For small or indie teams, a lean GDD is better than an encyclopedic document. Use Google Docs, Notion, or Confluence so everyone can collaborate and the document evolves with the project.

**On Level of Detail:** Write enough to communicate the intent clearly, but not so much that it stifles creativity during development. A GDD is not an immutable technical specification — it’s a living document.

**On Priority:** Always start with the Overview and the Core Loop. If you cannot clearly describe these two sections, the game concept still needs to be clarified before proceeding.
